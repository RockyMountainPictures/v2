The Camera Sensor

THE BASICS

Learn how modern camera sensors turn light into digital files.

Digital Revolution

Digital cameras have been around since 1988, and with the boom of technological innovation in the 21st century, they surpassed film in quality and popularity. But how does a camera sensor work?

(We are focusing on CMOS sensors because they are the most popular sensor type on the market.)

Inside a CMOS Sensor

CMOS sensors are made up of a slab of silicon with tiny boxes that make up your pixels. Each box has a photodiode to absorb the light, a colour filter to allow only one colour to pass through and hit the diode, and transistors and wiring to transmit the output voltage.

The Process

When the shutter button is pressed, the shutter opens and light floods through the lens and shines into the boxes. The colour filters do their job and the specific colour of light hits the photodiode. When the shutter closes, the photodiode converts the amount of light it absorbs into a specific voltage. That voltage is sent through the wiring to an Analog to Digital Converter (ADC). This converts the analog voltage into a digital signal. The ADC does this process one row of pixels at a time, which is called a rolling shutter. 

After the pixel get converted into a digital signal, they are stored on RAM until the photo is fully converted into a digital signal. The raw pixels then get compiled into an viewable image thanks to the main processor on the camera. The final file then gets transferred to the SD card or other medium of storage on the camera.
